Bob's Band

The Wailers

The Wailers began in 1963 as a vocal trio in a Trench Town yard — Bob, Peter Tosh, Bunny Wailer. Through Studio One, Lee Perry's Black Ark, and finally Island Records, that trio built the sound that made reggae a global language. Peter and Bunny walked away in 1974. What remained — the Barrett brothers' rhythm section, the keyboards, the I-Threes' harmonies — became the band that travelled the world with Bob until his death.

The Founders

The Original Trio (1963–74)

Three voices that met in a Kingston yard and built a sound out of doo-wop, ska, rocksteady, and the Rastafarian psalms drifting up from the camps. Together for eleven years; together on every record from The Wailing Wailers (1965) to Burnin' (1973).

Post-1974

The Wailers Band

When Peter and Bunny left, Bob built the touring band that took reggae to the stadiums. Aston and Carlton Barrett anchored the rhythm; Tyrone Downie and Earl Lindo on keys; Al Anderson and Junior Marvin trading lead guitar; Seeco Patterson on percussion as the elder of the room.

Backup Vocals

The I-Threes

Three Jamaican women who took over harmony duties from Peter and Bunny in 1974 — Rita Marley, Marcia Griffiths, and Judy Mowatt. All three were established solo artists in their own right. They sang on every Bob Marley & The Wailers record from Natty Dread (1974) onward.

The Producers

The Producers

The three men who built the rooms where the records were made — Coxsone Dodd at Studio One, Lee 'Scratch' Perry at Black Ark, and Chris Blackwell at Island Records. Three radically different studios, three radically different sounds, one continuous arc.
